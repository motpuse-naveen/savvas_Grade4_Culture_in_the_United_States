In This Text Set

This text set includes four informational texts and an interactive map. All focus on an aspect of culture and the way culture shapes our lives.

What Is Culture? is the anchor text for this set. Depending on the needs of your class, you may choose to assign What Is Culture? for independent reading, or you may choose to read it aloud with the class. Beginning with the anchor text will ground students in concepts and academic vocabulary that are key to discussing the Big Question. As students read the other texts in the set, they will compare their new understandings, as well as the structure and features of the text, with the anchor text.

After students read the anchor text, you may choose to assign specific texts or encourage students to choose the texts that appeal to them the most. Use the chart below to see where specific text features, text structures, and content topics appear across the texts in this set.

What Is Culture?

by Elena Knowles
Informational Text
Word Count: 547   Lexile: 760

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain how culture influences the way people modify and adapt to their environments.

  • Content Objective: Students will understand what culture is and how culture affects people’s lives.

  • Language Objective: Students will describe several aspects of culture, using examples.

  • Text Structure Objective: Students will understand how a description text structure lists features and examples to explain an idea.

  • Text Feature Objective: Students will understand how photographs illustrate complex ideas.

Summary

This text explains key aspects of culture, using a “cultural iceberg” model to explain how some parts of culture may be invisible to a casual observer.

Before Reading

Big Question: Remind students of the Big Question: What Is Culture? Ask: What would you like to learn about culture? What ideas do you have about what culture is?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words culture, tradition, and beliefs. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: Have you heard people talk about their culture? What were they talking about? Does your family have traditions, such as holidays you celebrate or special foods you eat? Can you give an example of a belief that you or someone you know has?

Oral Vocabulary Some of the vocabulary in What Is Culture? might be new or challenging for students. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: When you influence someone, you have an effect on them. Who are some people who influence your decisions? When you adapt to something, you get used to it. Have you ever needed to adapt to a new culture?

Vocabulary Four-square

Model creating a vocabulary four-square with students. Explain the four elements of the square: a definition of a word, an example of the word in a sentence, a synonym or antonym for the word, and an example. Say: How would you define culture? Could you use culture in a sentence? What is a synonym for culture? What is an example of a culture? Tell students to create a four-square for culture on a sheet of paper. A completed four-square might look like this:

During Reading

Elements Of Information Text

Text Structure Explain that this text has a descriptive text structure. Say: This kind of text describes or defines something by giving examples and details. Have students read page 3. Ask: How does the author define culture? (the way we live our lives) What are some examples of culture? (Possible responses: food, language, holidays, music, dance)

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Descriptive Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about descriptive structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “gives examples,” “explains something”

Text Features Say: This text uses photographs to illustrate different aspects of culture. Have students preview the photographs on pages 1-4 and use them to identify examples of culture. Then, point out the iceberg photograph on page 5. Say: The author compares different aspects of culture to the parts of an iceberg. How do you think culture is like an iceberg? How does the photograph help you understand this comparison?

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Photographs.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how pictures help readers understand the text. For example: “show examples,” “make a comparison”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read What Is Culture?, have them review their completed four-squares. Ask: Are there any squares you had a hard time filling in? If time permits, invite classmates to share their four-squares and add to them based on their classmates’ responses.

Display these images from What Is Culture? to help students think about vocabulary words they may have learned from the text, such as culture, tradition, and identify.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between What Is Culture? and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • What have you read about traditional foods, dance, or music? How did the [foods, dance, music] relate to a particular culture?
  • What Is Culture? describes how culture affects people’s beliefs or values. What else have you read about this topic? What are some examples of how culture affects people’s values?
  • The writer describes how cultures can have different expectations or unspoken rules. Have you read any books about people who need to adjust to a different culture? What happens in these books?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, display the Culture in the United States: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have students fill in the What Is Culture? row with something they learned about aspects of culture.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What is culture? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • What are some examples of culture in your life?
  • What parts of culture are easy to identify? What parts are harder to see?
  • What are some examples of cultures in the United States?

Write About The Big Question

Remind students of the description of the cultural iceberg in the text. Ask them to draw an iceberg diagram for culture in their lives. Remind them that the top part of the iceberg includes parts of their culture that are easy to see, such as holidays they celebrate and languages they speak. The other parts, such as how they talk to others, aren’t as easy to see.

Star Pictures

by Leslie Kellimon
Informational Text - Word Count: 43   Lexile: 420L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard: 1-ESS1-1. Use observations of the sun, moon, and stars to describe patterns that can be predicted.

  • Content Objective: Students will recognize that familiar shapes can be seen in the patterns of stars.

  • Language Objective: Students will name and describe at least one familiar shape that can be found in the night sky, i.e., the Big Dipper.

  • Text Structure Objective: Students will understand how a compare and contrast structure shows how things are alike or different.

  • Text Feature Objective: Students will understand how drawings help them understand an idea.

Summary

By looking at the night sky and connecting the stars with imaginary lines, we can see many familiar shapes.

Before Reading

Big Question: Remind students of the Big Question: What can we learn from observing the night sky? Ask: How do you think Star Pictures will help you think about the Big Question?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words stars and shapes. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class.

Ask: What shapes have you seen in the clouds? Have you ever made shapes by playing connect the dots? What shapes do you see when you observe the stars?

Oral Vocabulary Star Pictures includes some words that might be new or challenging to students. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: An astronomer is someone who studies the stars. If you were an astronomer, what would you try to learn about stars?

Word Web

Share a Word Web graphic organizer. To help students think about the relationships between words, guide them in creating a word web using the word star. When they read Star Pictures, encourage them to add words to their web that relate to the word star. A web for star might look like this:

[a word web with star in the center, and with night, sky, space, shapes, branching from it]

During Reading

Elements Of Information Text

Text Structure Explain to students that Star Pictures has a compare and contrast structure. Say: Sometimes writers explain things by showing how they are the same or different from other things. Direct students to pp. x-x of Star Pictures. Have volunteers share how comparing pictures of the stars with and without the shapes helps them understand how the shapes are formed.

Text Features Say: Pictures can help show how things are the same or different. Direct students to the pictures on pp. x-x. Ask: What does this first picture show? How is it the same as the next picture? How is it different? How do these pictures help you understand how people see shapes in the stars?

Track the Texts: Have students use the Observing the Night Sky: Track the Texts Track the Texts graphic organizer. As they read, have them fill in the Star Pictures column with the name of the structure, and any features they noticed, including pictures.

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Star Pictures, have them review their completed word webs. Ask: Are there any more words that you would like to add?

Display these images from Star Pictures to help students as they think about words related to star.

Discuss Across Text

Encourage students to make connections between Star Pictures and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • In Star Pictures, we learned that the stars in the sky can make shapes. What other facts have you read about stars?
  • Star Pictures describes one reason for looking at the night sky. What else have you read about why people look at the sky?
  • Think back to what you read about stars in In the Sky. What questions did you still have after reading In the Sky that were answered by Star Pictures?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Observing the Night Sky: Track the Texts graphic organizer. In the Star Pictures column, have them fill in the Big Ideas row with something they learned about stars.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What can we learn by observing the night sky? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • Why do you think people look for shapes in the night sky?
  • How might familiar shapes have you seen in the stars?

Write About The Big Question

Have students write about and draw a familiar shape they have seen in the stars. It could be a known constellation such as the Big Dipper, or a shape they see on their own.

Observing Moon Phases

Informational Video

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard: 1-ESS1-1. Use observations of the sun, moon, and stars to describe patterns that can be predicted.

  • Content Objective: Students will understand and differentiate between the phases of the moon.

  • Language Objective: Students will describe how the phases of the moon can be used to track time.

  • Text Structure Objective: Students will understand how a compare and contrast structure shows how things are alike or different.

  • Text Feature Objective: Students will understand how drawings help them understand an idea.

Summary

In this video, a teacher explains that the moon has different phases, and demonstrates what these phases look like.

Before Viewing

Big Question: Remind students of the Big Question: What can we learn from observing the night sky? Ask: How do you think Observing Moon Phases will add to your ideas about the Big Question?

Preview Vocabulary

During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: Draw a crescent shape for students. Say: A crescent is a shape that looks like this. Have you ever seen the moon look like this?

  • Why would it be easier to talk a walk at night under a full moon than under a new moon?
  • How does understanding the moon phases help you predict things about the moon?

Write About the Big Question

Provide students with blank calendars with a circle in each day, representing the moon. Have students shade the moon each day of the month to show the changing phases.

Name the Full Moons

by Thomas Taylor
Informational Text - Word Count: 432   Lexile: 510L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard Use observations of the sun, moon, and stars to describe patterns that can be predicted.

  • Content Objective Students will learn the name of the full moon for each month of the year.

  • Language Objective Students will describe the connection between the moon names and the time of year.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a descriptive text structure describes things and ideas.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how photographs help them understand what a writer is describing.

Summary

Did you ever wonder how the Harvest Moon got its name? This text shares the Native American origins for the name of the full moon of each month.

Before Reading

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: Why do people observe the night sky? Ask: How do you think Name the Full Moons will help you think about the Big Question?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words full moon, moon, and season. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class.

Ask: How often do you see a full moon in the sky? What does it look like to you? Does the moon have a different feeling to it in different seasons?

Oral Vocabulary

Much of the vocabulary in Name the Full Moons might be familiar to most students, but other texts in this set include words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: When we observe something, we look at it carefully. What do you notice when you observe a full moon?

Word Web

Share a Word Web graphic organizer. To help students think about the relationships between words, guide them in creating a word web using the word moon. When they read Name the Full Moons, encourage them to add words to their web that relate to the word moon. A web for moon might look like this:

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain to students that Name the Full Moons is written with a descriptive text structure. Say: When writers want to explain something, they describe what it is or how it works. Project the text or direct students to p. 7 of Name the Full Moons on their devices. Say: As writers describe something they give details about that thing. The more details the writer gives, the better you understand. Have volunteers share details from the text that describe how the full moon in April got its name.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Descriptive Structure." Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about descriptive structure, and how it helps them understand the writer's ideas. For example: “gives details," "explains something"

Text Features Say: Using pictures can help explain an idea. Point to the photograph on p. 6. Ask: What does this picture show? How does this picture help you understand why the full moon in March is called the Worm Moon? (Possible response: It shows worms.)

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write "Pictures". Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how pictures help readers understand the text. For example: "show what the words tell about."

Track the Texts: Have students use the Observing the Night Sky: Track the Texts graphic organizer. As they read, have them fill in the Name the Full Moons column with the name of the text structure, and any text features they noticed, including photographs

After students have read Name the Full Moons, have them review their completed word webs. Ask: Are there any more words that you would like to add?

Display these images from Name the Full Moons to help students as they think about words related to moon.

Encourage students to make connections between Name the Full Moons and other texts they have read on related subjects.

Through a Telescope

by Burgess Clevelittle
Informational Text - Word Count: 99   Lexile: 330L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard: 1-ESS1-1. Use observations of the sun, moon, and stars to describe patterns that can be predicted.

  • Content Objective: Students will understand how a telescope helps to see objects in the sky.

  • Language Objective: Students will compare and contrast what can be seen with and without a telescope.

  • Text Structure Objective: Students will understand how a compare and contrast structure shows how things are alike or different.

  • Text Feature Objective: Students will understand how drawings help them understand an idea.

Summary

This informational text shows, through text and images, how looking through a telescope makes distant objects in the night sky easier to see, and with greater detail.

Before Reading

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: What can we learn from observing the night sky? Ask: How do you think Through a Telescope will relate to the Big Question?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words stars, telescope, moon, and planet. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class.

Ask: What can you see when you look up at the night sky? Can you see the moon? Can you see any planets? Have you ever used a telescope to see far away things?

Oral Vocabulary

Through a Telescope includes vocabulary that might be new or challenging for students. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: When we observe something, we look at it carefully. How does making something look bigger help you observe it?

Share a Word Web graphic organizer. To help students think about the relationships between words, guide them in creating a word web using the word telescope. When they read Through a Telescope, encourage them to add words to their web that relate to the word telescope. A web for telescope might look like this:

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain to students that Through the Telescope has a compare and contrast structure. Say: Sometimes writers explain things by showing how they are the same or different from other things. Direct students to pp. x-x of Through the Telescope. Have volunteers share how showing the moon far away and up close explains what a telescope does.

Text Features Say: Pictures can help show how things are the same or different. Direct students to the pictures on pp. x-x. Ask: What does this first picture show? How is it different from the next picture? How does comparing these pictures help you understand the job a telescope does?

Track the Texts: Have students use the Observing the Night Sky: Track the Texts graphic organizer. As they watch the video, have them fill in the Through the Telescope column with the name of the structure, and any features they noticed, including drawings.

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Through a Telescope, have them review their completed word webs. Ask: Are there any more words that you would like to add?

Display these images from Through a Telescope to help students as they think about words related to telescope.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between Name the Full Moons and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • In Through a Telescope, we learned that some things that are very far away can be seen with a telescope. What other facts have you read about observing objects in the sky?
  • Think back to what you read about the night sky in In the Sky. What more did you learn about the sky from reading Through a Telescope?

Track the Texts After the discussion, display the Observing the Night Sky: Track the Texts graphic organizer. In the Through a Telescope column, have them fill in the Big Ideas row with something they learned about using telescopes to study the sky.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What can we learn by observing the night sky? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • Have you ever looked through a telescope? What did you see?
  • What would you like to try to see through a telescope?

Write About the Big Question

Have students look at an object, such as a map, a poster, or a photograph from across the room, and then up close. Have them list details they could see from across the room, and details they could see up close.

Observing the Night Sky



Wrap It Up

Now that the class has read some or all of the texts in this set, it is time to pull together all the information they have learned from their reading in order to explore and share their evolving understanding of the Big Question.

Vocabulary Round-Up

Word Sort Have students review the word webs they have created for the texts they have read, and then do a word sort, identifying new connections between the words using all the information they now have from their reading. For example, students might connect the words crescent, full moon, gibbous, phase, and moon, as they're all related to phases of the moon.

Ask students to share their word sorts and explain why they connected the words the way they did. Students may sort words based on their own experiences, so accept all reasonable explanations for the connections they make.

Discuss the Text Set's Big Question

Guide a class discussion around the Big Question: Why do people observe the night sky? Encourage students to bring in knowledge they have learned from all the texts they have read in the text set, using as many of the words from their word webs as they can.

  • What did you already know about things in the sky before you began reading this text set?
  • Now that you’ve read a lot of books about the sky, what new things did you learn?
  • What about the night sky would you like to learn more about?
  • Which texts were most useful in helping you answer the Big Question?

Culminating Project

Have students apply what they’ve learned from the text set to demonstrate and enhance their understanding. Review the Project Rubric with students, explaining in detail the expectations for the project.

PROJECT: Sky Tour!

Have students draw a picture of the night sky. Ask them to make sure they include at least three objects they read about in this text set.

Have them plan and practice how they would give a tour of the night sky. What would they say about each thing in the sky? What interesting information from their reading would they share?

Students can refer to what they wrote in Write About the Big Question for each text to aid their thinking and planning. They may also use the Project Template to help them organize and clarify their thinking as they plan their projects. Encourage them to use the vocabulary they’ve learned from the texts in their tours.

When they are finished planning their tours, provide students with a variety of settings in which they can share their tours, such as in a small group setting, for the whole class, or with a single partner.

Discussion Tips

An important part of building a vibrant classroom culture is creating an atmosphere in which all students feel empowered to participate in powerful classroom conversation. You may have noticed that your students who are naturally extroverted often participate freely in a whole-class discussion, while those who are more introverted or, perhaps, who come from cultures that respect different classroom norms, may need encouragement to share. You can use sentence prompts like these to invite all students into the conversation:

“That’s so interesting … please tell me more!”

“Can someone explain that in a different way?”

“What are some examples of that?”

“Can someone add on to that idea?”

Sharing and discussing ideas will often involve agreeing and/or disagreeing with what others say. As students listen carefully and respond thoughtfully to their classmates’ ideas, encourage them to express themselves in respectful and constructive ways. You may wish to post these sentence starters prominently, so students can refer to them often:

“I agree, and I also think fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I mostly agree, but fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I like what you just said because fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I respectfully disagree because fill in the blank line__________ .”
